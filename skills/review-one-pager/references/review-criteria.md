# One-Pager Review Criteria

Use these criteria to evaluate each section of the one-pager being reviewed.

---

## Rating Tags

- ✅ **Strong** — Section is specific, honest, and complete. No major gaps.
- ⚠️ **Needs work** — Section exists but has vague claims, missing data, or weak framing.
- ❌ **Missing or critical gap** — Section is absent or so thin it's not useful.

---

## Section-by-Section Criteria

### 1. Problem Statement

**Strong looks like:**
- Names a specific user segment (role, company type, workflow context)
- States the consequence of the problem staying unsolved
- Cites at least one real signal (support tickets, churn data, NPS verbatim, interview quote, sales data)
- Is 3–5 sentences — not a paragraph essay

**Common weaknesses:**
- "Users struggle with X" — who are these users exactly?
- No data or signal — just assertion
- Describes a solution, not a problem
- Too long — becomes a problem analysis doc, not a statement

---

### 2. Why Now

**Strong looks like:**
- Names a specific external or internal change that creates urgency
- Links the change to real consequences (customer churn risk, competitive gap, expiring opportunity)
- Is honest if there's no strong "why now" — and flags it as a risk

**Common weaknesses:**
- "Now is a good time" — this says nothing
- Conflates "we've been asked to do this" with genuine market urgency
- Missing entirely (section skipped or merged into Problem)

---

### 3. Proposed Solution

**Strong looks like:**
- Describes the experience from the user's perspective — what they do, what changes
- Avoids technical architecture and implementation detail
- Makes clear why this is meaningfully better than the current state
- 4–6 sentences — not a feature list

**Common weaknesses:**
- Describes the feature ("we will build X") rather than the user experience ("users will be able to...")
- Too abstract — no tangible description of what changes for the user
- Reads like engineering requirements, not a PM pitch

---

### 4. Goals / Non-Goals

**Strong looks like:**
- In-scope goals are specific and bounded — not "improve user experience"
- Non-goals are explicit about what is NOT being solved and briefly why
- 2–4 items each — not exhaustive lists

**Common weaknesses:**
- Goals are vague ("make it easier for users")
- Non-goals are missing entirely — this is a major red flag
- Non-goals are just a negative restatement of goals, not actual scope boundaries

---

### 5. Target Users

**Strong looks like:**
- Primary beneficiary is named specifically (role, company type, workflow)
- Explains why the problem is acute for this segment specifically
- Secondary users mentioned if relevant, but not conflated with primary

**Common weaknesses:**
- "All users" or "our customers" — too broad
- Describes demographics without context about why this matters for this problem
- No explanation of why this segment is the right primary target

---

### 6. Success Metrics

**Strong looks like:**
- One north star metric that proves success, with a timeframe
- A leading indicator measurable within 30 days of launch
- A guardrail metric that must not get worse
- Metrics are measurable, not aspirational ("increase NPS" vs. "+5 NPS points in 60 days")

**Common weaknesses:**
- List of 5+ metrics — no clear north star
- Metrics are vague ("improve engagement", "increase usage")
- No timeframe attached to any metric
- No guardrail — implies nothing could go wrong

---

### 7. Risks & Open Questions

**Strong looks like:**
- Names 2–3 real, honest risks — not sanitized
- Includes at least one assumption risk ("we assume users want X — this is unvalidated")
- Includes technical, adoption, and/or competitive risks as applicable

**Common weaknesses:**
- "Risks: none identified" — not credible
- Only technical risks listed — no adoption or assumption risks
- Risks stated without any mitigation or follow-up plan

---

### 8. Rough Scope

**Strong looks like:**
- 3–4 named milestones, each approximately 2 sprints long
- Each milestone specifies what ships and what outcome or signal it validates
- Milestones are independently shippable or testable — not just arbitrary time slices
- Acknowledges the biggest open risk to the milestone plan explicitly
- If fewer than 3 milestones are used, explains why the work is genuinely self-contained

**Common weaknesses:**
- A single t-shirt size (S/M/L/XL) with no breakdown of what ships when
- Milestones that are just phases of build ("design", "build", "test") rather than shippable slices
- No stated goal per milestone — milestones without outcomes are just deadlines
- Missing the key assumption that, if wrong, changes the entire sequencing
- More than 4 milestones without acknowledging this may warrant splitting into separate initiatives
- Milestone 1 is too large to validate any signal within 2 sprints

---

### 9. GTM / Validation Plan

**Strong looks like:**
- Internal dogfooding team named specifically
- 5–10 real customer names or a well-defined segment for friendly beta
- Minimum signal defined — something observable, not "positive feedback"

**Common weaknesses:**
- "We'll do a beta" — no specifics
- Minimum signal is vague ("good response from customers")
- GTM plan is a launch checklist rather than a validation strategy
- Missing entirely — often the most skipped section

---

## Overall Verdict Rubric

After scoring all sections, give an overall verdict:

| Rating | Criteria |
|--------|----------|
| **Ready to share** | 7+ sections at ✅ Strong, no ❌ gaps |
| **Almost there** | 5–6 sections at ✅, 1–2 ⚠️ that can be addressed quickly |
| **Needs significant work** | 3+ sections at ⚠️ or any ❌ in sections 1, 4, 6, or 7 |
| **Start over** | Core problem or solution unclear, or 4+ sections ❌ |
